(1)Omitted variable

In a traditional wage determination function, more demographic factors (e.g. marital status, religion, region of living) should be involved.

Besides, unobservable capacities(e.g. IQ) related to the independent variables (Schooling) are also omitted from the function.

(2)Misspecification

Not likely since the coefficient of the quadratic terms is not significant.

(3)Errors in variables

Measurement error is always there but we are not sure about its structure in this case.

E.g. the retrospective AHE could be inprecise. But as long as it's a classical measurement error, i.e. w is purely random, the estimator of the coefficient is still unbiased (but less efficient).

(4)Sample selection

Survivorship Bias: What if workers earn less than some value tend to starve to death?

(5)Simultaneous causality

Not likely because wage cannot affect age.

(6)Inconsistency of OLS standard errors

Not applicable. We have employed the robust std. error for the cross-sectional data, which means there will not be the heteroskedasticity or "correlation of the error term across observations" problems.

Internal validation

An obvious omitted variable is the average test score of the class. There are reasons to believe that the higher the mean sore is, the higher the rating will be.

It's possible that school may assign goodlooking teachers to teach interesting courses as some kind of ads.

(2)Errors in variables

There seems to be serious measurement error. The marginal attribution of beauty of female teachers(0.107) is much lower than that of the male(0.238). Maybe girls who prefer handsome guys take up the most part of the class. In this case, what "beauty" means is closely related to the gender of the teacher.

Teachers with low ratings may refuse to show their pics on their webpages in case being recognized and laughed at by students.

Even more complicated and more general, students who care about the appearances of the teachers may only choose the courses taught by goodlooking teachers.
